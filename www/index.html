State-of-the-art functional magnetic resonance imaging data acquired with a Siemens Magnetom 7-Tesla scanner.

Rich stimulus

Participants listened to a narrated, audio-only version of the two-hour Hollywood movie "Forrest Gump". The stimulus is aligned to the commercial DVD release to facilitate further annotation.

View details »

High-resolution anatomical images

T1 and T2-weighted anatomical images with a voxel-size of 0.7 mm for all participants.

View details »

Natural brain activity samples

Possibly the largest consecutive sample of brain imaging data recorded during natural language processing. FMRI data corrected for EPI distortions and ready for analysis.

View details »

Vascular structure imaging

High-resolution blood vessel localizers. Arterography for the field-of-view of the functional brain scans, and a full-brain venography.

View details »

Versatile noise estimates

Simultaneous measurements of cardiac and respiratory trace during fMRI recording. Motion estimates extracted from fMRI data prior distortion correction. Independent evaluation of technical noise with a full-length phantom recording.

Tweet

The human brain is designed for processing vast amounts of input that are continuously gathered through the senses. But most experiments study the brain with simplified stimuli that do not resemble the complexity of a natural environment — a mismatch that needs to be addressed if we want to completely understand how the brain works. This project aims to provide essential components to enable inter-disciplinary research to study the brain's natural behavior and the versatile interactions of concurrent cognitive processes in everyday life.

Goals

We promote the use of natural stimulation in brain research. Use these data for a scientific study, as a benchmark, or to develop educational material. Extend the dataset with original or derived data to enable new applications.

Read more »

Resources

We provide extensive functional brain imaging data from natural stimulation, a rich set of auxiliary data, such as structural brain scans, measurements of physiological and technical confounds, as well as stimulus annotations.

See overview »

Access

All data and software components are publicly available. There is no required sign-up or registration procedure. Data are released under an extremely permissive license and can be used by academic and commercial entities.

Project results

Inter-subject response similarities. Distributed activation patterns and voxel timeseries.

BOLD time series correlation and pattern similarity estimated from representational similarity analysis on anatomically aligned data reveal consistent brain responses to quasi natural auditory stimulation across all participants. Areas of maximum similarity in both hemispheres are centered on the anterolateral portion of the Planum Temporale — part of the auditory ventrolateral pathway and known to be involved in processing complex sounds and speech.

Hanke, M., Baumgartner, F.J., Ibe, P., Kaule, F.R., Pollmann, S., Speck, O., Zinke, W. & Stadler, J. (in press). A high-resolution 7-Tesla fMRI dataset from complex natural stimulation with an audio movie. Scientific Data.

About this project

This project is an open invitation to anyone to participate in a decentralized effort to explore the opportunities of open science in neuroimaging. It aims to document how much (scientific) value can be generated out of a data release by publication of scientific findings derived from this dataset, algorithms and methods evaluated on this dataset, and/or extensions of this dataset by acquisition and incorporation of new data.
